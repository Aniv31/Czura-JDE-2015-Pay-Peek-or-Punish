# Motivation

Microcredit has generally been a success in promoting credit access, though there is documented evidence that micro groups formed have been brutal when a member defaults, from burning down their homes to complete ostracisations.

Czura has two things she sets out to do in this paper:

1. Test whether there is evidence for this excessive punishment.
2. See if a formal game theoretic model of microfinance incentivizes excessive punishment.

She achieves the former through an experiment among low-literate, actual borrowers of an SHG in Bihar. As Czura notes repeatedly, this seeks to enhance the external validity of the paper.

# The Experiment

- Each participant receives ₹100, which is automatically invested.
- If the investment succeeds (probability 5/6), they receive ₹250.
- If the investment fails (probability 1/6), they receive ₹10.
- The stakes are substantial for the participants.

The returns are realized afterwards.

Two variations of the game are used as treatment arms:

- A peeking game where participants can peek at their peer's returns.
- A punishment game where participants can punish their peers.
- A game where participants can both peek and punish.

Everybody first played the standard game. Which treatment game a participant played was randomized.

Participants answered questions related to every possible scenario. Apparently, they were able to understand the instructions.

# Theory and Results

Given that when both players don't repay they will be punished by the bank, if only one repays he also has to pay for his peer (joint liability in SHGs), for risk-neutral players both players will not repay in equilibrium.

Choosing the opposite strategies is better in both simultaneous and sequential games: (repay, default) and (default, repay). If my peer repays, it is better I take advantage of it and make him pay my loan too. If my peer defaults, it is better I pay and am saved from action by the bank.

If I am risk averse though, I would be hesitant to default in any case (I really want to avoid punishment by the bank), so repayment rates would be higher.

What we really observe is high repayment rates (93 percent). Maybe the peers were really risk averse? But calculated risk aversion (through lottery) doesn't explain these rates.

Similarly, why would I want to punish or peek at my peer? It doesn't increase my returns and it costs money to punish them, yet we see high punishment and peeking rates across different games.

The paper doesn't discuss the mechanism by which this occurs, though they suggest excessive indoctrination due to the SHGs mottos and messaging.
